The UK Estonian Choir was formed as a project choir by conductor Kristi Jagodin (conductor of the London Estonian Mixed Choir) to participate at the Estonian Song Festival in Summer 2019 in Tallinn. The choir is comprised of singers of different nationalities from across the UK and practices once a month in London and Scotland.

The Estonian Song Celebration is a tradition that dates back to the year 1869. In almost 150 years, the song celebration has had a significant effect on the development of Estonian identity and politics. The tradition has enriched the Estonian language and culture. More than once has it encouraged the population to oppose the occupying forces while pursuing the wish for freedom and self-determination.

Nowadays, the song celebration is organised once every five years. In the early days of July, each celebration will bring almost 25,000 performers to Tallinn. Not everyone can perform at the celebration – the performers need to know the concert’s programme, which means that each choir has to learn about 15 songs in Estonian. Important parts of the celebration are the performers’ procession from the centre of Tallinn to the Song Festival Grounds (about 5 km), lighting the flame of the celebration, two days of concerts and the closing ceremony. All of it will be preceded by huge choir rehearsals at the festival grounds. Participation in the festival is therefore mentally and physically challenging, but the result is a miracle that brings the whole nation together and provides an extraordinary experience to everyone who chooses to take a part in it.

In 2019 we’ll have the 27th song celebration entitled Minu arm [My Love]. Choirs from the Estonian diaspora and foreign collectives are all welcome to participate.

For a more thorough overview of the history visit the Song Celebration 2019 website.

Source: Song Celebration 2019

The venue of the Song Celebration is the Tallinn Song Festival Grounds (Tallinna Lauluväljak) and has hosted the celebration since 1960.

The Song Festival Grounds fit up to 100,000 people.
The performers sing under a constructed arch
that carries sound over the whole spectator area.

At the beginning of every celebration, a fire is lit on top of the 42-meter tower that offers fantastic views over the sea and land.

Each Song and Dance Celebration involves a procession, which passes through Tallinn to the Song Festival Grounds, uniting the Dance and the Song Celebrations.

The Dance Celebration takes place during the same weekend, with 8,500-10,000 beautifully clad folk dancers creating patterns on Kalevi stadium.
